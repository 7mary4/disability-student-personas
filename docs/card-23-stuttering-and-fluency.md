# Stuttering & Fluency

**Category:** Speech & Language  
**IDEA/504 Eligibility:** Speech or Language Impairment (SLI)

## Overview
Stuttering is a fluency disorder characterized by disruptions in speech production — repetitions, prolongations, and blocks — that can significantly affect communication, academic participation, and social confidence. The emotional and social impact of stuttering is often more impairing than the speech itself. Teachers who create low-pressure, patient communication environments make a profound difference.

---

## Elementary (K–5)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Repetitions ("I-I-I want to..."), prolongations ("Ssssnake"), or silent blocks before words
- Avoids oral participation; declines to answer even when they clearly know
- May use circumlocution — substituting easier words to avoid difficult ones
- Increased disfluency under pressure (being called on suddenly, timed tasks)

**Student strengths:**
- Often thoughtful, determined, and persistent communicators
- Strong listeners and empathizers as a result of their own communication experiences
- Many celebrated communicators and leaders who stutter — the disability does not limit intelligence or leadership

**Recommended accommodations:**
- NEVER finish the student's words or sentences — this is invalidating and unhelpful
- Maintain natural eye contact and patient body language; don't rush or look away
- Allow alternative participation formats (written response, pre-prepared answer)
- Do not call on the student without warning; use voluntary participation
- Address teasing immediately and privately with a clear classroom norm

**Assistive technology:**
- SpeechEasy or electronic fluency devices (prescribed by SLP)
- Speech practice apps for at-home fluency work
- Text-to-speech for situations where verbal response is too stressful

**UDL strategies:**
- Create a classroom culture where all communication styles are respected
- Provide multiple ways to participate: written, drawn, verbal, digital
- Never use timed oral activities that add pressure to fluency

**IEP considerations:**
- SLP services are the primary intervention — direct fluency therapy
- Annual goal: Student will use fluency strategies (e.g., easy onset, cancellation) during X out of Y classroom speaking opportunities
- Social-emotional goals should address confidence and self-advocacy about stuttering
- Accommodation: student may request written alternative to oral presentations

**Who to collaborate with:** Speech-Language Pathologist, School Counselor

**Family partnership tip:** Never tell your child to slow down, take a breath, or start over. These well-intentioned suggestions increase anxiety and worsen stuttering. Instead, model slow, relaxed speech yourself and give your full, patient attention when they speak.

## Middle School (6–8)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Increasing avoidance of speaking due to peer awareness and social anxiety
- May have developed significant speech avoidance strategies that limit participation
- Academic participation in discussions is much lower than knowledge would suggest
- May be experiencing bullying or social isolation related to stuttering

**Student strengths:**
- Often deeply empathetic and strong advocates for others
- Thoughtful, careful communicators who choose words deliberately
- Often develop exceptional written communication as an alternative strength

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Allow written alternatives for all oral participation requirements
- Do not penalize for choosing not to speak in high-pressure situations
- Provide advance notice of any required oral activities
- Flexible format for book reports, presentations, and class participation grades
- Address any teasing or bullying directly and promptly

**Assistive technology:**
- Voluntary use of fluency apps as student chooses
- Voice recording tools for practicing before presentations
- Text-to-speech for situations where live speech is too anxiety-provoking

**UDL strategies:**
- Destigmatize stuttering through inclusive classroom culture
- Provide all students with multiple participation options — benefits everyone
- Teach the class about communication diversity if student consents

**IEP considerations:**
- SLP goals may address both fluency techniques AND communication confidence/attitudes
- Social-emotional goal: Student will use at least one self-advocacy statement about stuttering in X out of Y new adult interactions
- Academic participation accommodation must be explicitly documented in the IEP
- Address bullying in IEP as an educational access issue if it is occurring

**Who to collaborate with:** Speech-Language Pathologist, School Counselor

**Family partnership tip:** Connect your student with the National Stuttering Association's teen programs — meeting other teens who stutter and learning from adults who stutter and thrive is transformative for middle school confidence.

## High School (9–12)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Strategic avoidance of courses with heavy oral requirements (debate, drama, presentation-heavy classes)
- May have already developed strong written communication as their primary academic tool
- Anxiety about job interviews, college interviews, and post-secondary presentations
- Has a well-developed identity around stuttering — positive or still developing

**Student strengths:**
- Often exceptional written communicators
- Highly reflective and empathetic
- Many adults who stutter become powerful advocates and communicators in their fields

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Written or recorded alternatives for all oral assessments
- No forced public speaking; all oral participation is voluntary
- Extended time if anxiety is affecting communication in assessment contexts
- Self-advocacy support for college interview preparation
- Connect student with stuttering support and advocacy communities

**Assistive technology:**
- Speech practice apps for self-directed fluency work
- Video recording tools for self-review of communication goals
- Text alternatives for high-stakes communication contexts

**UDL strategies:**
- Provide all students with written and digital communication options as standard practice
- Value and celebrate diverse communication styles explicitly
- Teach students about famous communicators who stutter as part of disability awareness

**IEP considerations:**
- Transition planning must address post-secondary communication contexts: college interviews, workplace communication
- Annual goal: Student will independently use self-disclosure strategies about stuttering in X post-secondary simulated situations
- Summary of Performance should include communication profile for disability services offices
- Consider whether SLP services transition to coaching model for post-secondary preparation

**Who to collaborate with:** Speech-Language Pathologist, College Guidance Counselor

**Family partnership tip:** Research the National Stuttering Association's resources for teens — there are scholarships, camps, and community programs specifically for young people who stutter. Connection with this community is often life-changing.
