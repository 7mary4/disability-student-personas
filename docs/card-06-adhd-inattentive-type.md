# ADHD - Inattentive Type

**Category:** Attention & Executive Function  
**IDEA/504 Eligibility:** Other Health Impairment (OHI) — ADHD is specifically named under IDEA OHI

## Overview
ADHD Inattentive Type is characterized primarily by difficulty sustaining attention, following through on tasks, organizing work, and avoiding distraction — without the prominent hyperactivity seen in other ADHD presentations. These students are often overlooked because they are quiet and compliant, yet their academic performance is undermined by persistent attentional challenges.

---

## Elementary (K–5)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Frequently off-task during independent work; stares into space
- Loses books, folders, assignments, and materials regularly
- Misses steps in multi-part instructions even when given slowly
- Starts tasks but does not finish; work is incomplete at bell

**Student strengths:**
- Often highly creative and imaginative (their "daydreaming" is active thinking)
- Kind, empathetic, and well-liked by peers
- Can hyperfocus deeply on topics of genuine interest

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Preferential seating near the teacher, away from high-traffic areas
- Break long tasks into small, chunked steps with check-ins
- Provide visual checklists for multi-step tasks and routines
- Allow movement breaks every 15–20 minutes
- Use timers (visual or auditory) to support task completion

**Assistive technology:**
- Time Timer app or physical Time Timer clock
- Google Classroom task lists with checkboxes
- ClassDojo or Class Charts for behavior/task monitoring (teacher-facing)

**UDL strategies:**
- Use "parking lot" sticky notes so students can capture ideas without derailing focus
- Incorporate movement into instruction (stand-and-share, gallery walks)
- Provide immediate, specific feedback to redirect attention positively

**IEP considerations:**
- Present levels must include data on task completion rate and attention duration
- Consider eligibility under Other Health Impairment (OHI) — ADHD is specifically named in IDEA under OHI
- Annual goal: Student will complete X% of in-class assignments within allotted time using a visual checklist
- Coordinate with parents about medication management and its effects on school performance

**Who to collaborate with:** School Counselor (for organizational coaching), Pediatrician or prescribing physician (re: medication)

**Family partnership tip:** Help your child build a "launch pad" — one spot by the door where everything needed for school lives. Reducing morning chaos reduces the chance items get forgotten.

## Middle School (6–8)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Assignment notebook or planner is rarely filled in correctly
- Turns in incomplete work or forgets to submit digital assignments
- Struggles to initiate long-term projects; starts the night before
- Appears present in class but retains little from lectures without notes

**Student strengths:**
- Highly creative; often the "idea generator" in group projects
- Strong in hands-on, project-based learning
- Curious and intrinsically motivated when content connects to interests

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Teacher checks planner or digital task list daily
- Email or digital notification reminders for upcoming deadlines
- Allow assignment submission by email if paper copy is lost
- Provide structured note templates for all lectures
- Check-in with student at start and end of each class period

**Assistive technology:**
- Google Calendar with parent-teacher shared access for deadlines
- Todoist or Microsoft To-Do for task management
- Notability or OneNote for organized note-taking

**UDL strategies:**
- Teach organizational strategies explicitly — do not assume students develop them naturally
- Use project management scaffolds (backwards planning, milestone check-ins) for any long assignment
- Allow students to submit in-progress work for feedback before final deadline

**IEP considerations:**
- Address organization and homework completion explicitly as IEP goals — not just academic content goals
- Annual goal: Student will record and complete X% of assignments using a digital planner with weekly teacher check-in
- Consider counseling as a related service focused on organizational and study skills
- Ensure all teachers on the team have read the IEP accommodations — consistency is critical

**Who to collaborate with:** School Counselor, Advisory/Homeroom Teacher (for daily check-in role)

**Family partnership tip:** Create a homework routine at the same time and place every day. Ask "What's due tomorrow?" at dinner — a consistent daily prompt is more powerful than occasional reminders.

## High School (9–12)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Has multiple missing assignments and zero-grade penalties despite knowing the content
- Starts studying but gets sidetracked; falls behind on independent coursework
- Frequently overwhelmed by large projects; paralyzed rather than productive
- May have given up on some classes and overcompensates in areas of interest

**Student strengths:**
- Often deeply knowledgeable about specific areas of passion
- Creative and innovative when engaged
- Responds strongly to mentorship and authentic relationship with teachers

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Individualized deadline extensions with prior arrangement (not ad hoc)
- Late work policies that allow submission without catastrophic grade penalties
- Weekly check-in with a trusted adult (counselor, case manager, mentor teacher)
- Visual project planning tools provided for all major assignments
- Reduced homework load with emphasis on in-class demonstration of mastery

**Assistive technology:**
- Notion or Trello for project and deadline tracking
- Forest app or Focus@Will for distraction management during work sessions
- Remind or Canvas notifications for assignment reminders

**UDL strategies:**
- Allow flexible deadlines with proactive planning agreements rather than reactive extensions
- Provide choice in assignment formats to increase buy-in and initiation
- Build in self-assessment check-ins midway through all projects

**IEP considerations:**
- Transition goals must address executive function explicitly for post-secondary success
- Consider whether student's credit deficits are disability-related and address in IEP compensatory strategies
- Annual goal: Student will use a digital planning system to submit X% of assignments on time across all classes
- Address late-work policies and grading accommodations explicitly in the IEP document

**Who to collaborate with:** Guidance Counselor (credit recovery planning), Case Manager or Advisory Teacher

**Family partnership tip:** Help your teen identify their peak focus time of day and protect it for homework. Trying to study when their attention is at its lowest is a setup for frustration.
