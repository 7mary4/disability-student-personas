# Trauma-Informed Needs

**Category:** Emotional & Behavioral  
**IDEA/504 Eligibility:** Emotional Disturbance (ED) or Other Health Impairment (OHI) — identify the educational impact and underlying eligibility

## Overview
Students with trauma-related needs have experienced adverse events — abuse, neglect, violence, loss, community trauma, or instability — that affect how their nervous system responds to stress, relationships, and the school environment. Trauma-informed teaching prioritizes safety, consistency, predictability, and relationship as the foundation for learning. Educators are not therapists, but teacher responses to trauma-related behavior have a profound effect on whether students heal and learn.

---

## Elementary (K–5)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Strong startle response to unexpected sounds, touch, or sudden movement
- Hypervigilance — frequently scanning the room, watching the door, unable to settle
- Avoidance or shutdown when certain topics arise (family, home, particular subjects)
- Emotional dysregulation that appears disproportionate to the trigger

**Student strengths:**
- Often highly attuned to social and emotional cues — a strength that can support empathy
- Demonstrates remarkable resilience and survival skills developed through adversity
- When trusting relationships are established, shows capacity for deep engagement and loyalty

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Establish a predictable, consistent daily routine and warn of any changes in advance
- Create a designated safe space in or near the classroom for when dysregulated
- Use a private non-verbal check-in system (a feelings card, a color scale) at the start of each day
- Avoid public correction, sarcasm, or raised voices; use calm, private redirects
- Do not require the student to discuss home or family in class activities; offer neutral alternatives

**Assistive technology:**
- Calming apps (Calm, Headspace for Kids, Breathe2Relax) for self-regulation during escalation
- Visual schedule app to support predictability and reduce transition anxiety
- Weighted lap pad or sensory fidget tools (with OT guidance) for self-regulation at the desk

**UDL strategies:**
- Build a predictable structure into every lesson: consistent opening, activity, and closing routines
- Use choice-giving throughout the day to provide safe experiences of agency and control
- Incorporate movement, creative arts, and hands-on activities to engage multiple regulatory pathways

**IEP considerations:**
- Trauma alone does not create eligibility — identify the educational impact (ED, OHI, SLD) and document clearly
- Any behavior plan (BIP) must be trauma-informed: punitive consequences for trauma responses are ineffective and re-traumatizing
- Annual goal: Student will independently use a pre-taught calming strategy within 2 minutes of an identified trigger in 4 of 5 observed opportunities
- Mental health services coordination: IEP team should document referral to school-based mental health services or outside therapist

**Who to collaborate with:** School Counselor, Social Worker

**Family partnership tip:** You do not need to share the details of the trauma with the school — only what helps the teacher respond appropriately. A simple note such as "My child is working with a therapist on managing stress; predictable routines and calm responses to big emotions are most helpful" is often enough.

## Middle School (6–8)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Reactive aggression in response to perceived threats (being bumped, looked at wrong)
- Academic performance is wildly inconsistent with the student's actual cognitive ability
- Avoids or tests adult relationships — doesn't believe adults will stay or be safe
- May use substances or engage in risk-taking behaviors outside school

**Student strengths:**
- Has survived genuinely hard circumstances — strength and resilience are real
- Responds powerfully to authentic connection with an adult who doesn't give up
- Creative self-expression when safe enough to try

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Eliminate humiliation-based discipline (public shaming, removal in front of peers)
- Use restorative, not punitive, responses to behavioral incidents
- Daily check-in with one consistent trusted adult
- Flexible academic expectations during periods of acute crisis at home
- Mandated reporter obligations handled with sensitivity and transparency

**Assistive technology:**
- Private journaling or audio recording for processing
- Online resources about trauma for student's own understanding (age-appropriate)
- Calming apps available on student device

**UDL strategies:**
- Use trauma-responsive practices school-wide, not just for this student
- Allow creative and physical expression as valid academic engagement
- Build in student agency and leadership to counteract learned helplessness

**IEP considerations:**
- Avoid diagnosing or labeling trauma in the IEP without family consent and clinical evaluation
- Annual goal should focus on regulation, trust-building behaviors, and academic engagement
- School counselor and outside mental health connection are critical components
- Document any punitive school practices that may violate IDEA protections for trauma-related behavior

**Who to collaborate with:** School Counselor, School Social Worker

**Family partnership tip:** Your child's school behaviors may look like defiance but are survival responses. Share what you know about triggers with the teacher in a private conversation — this context can transform how your child is treated at school.

## High School (9–12)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Complex, layered trauma history that intersects with poverty, racism, or instability
- Has been involved with child welfare, probation, or housing instability
- Academic engagement is possible when safety and trust are established
- Self-protective behaviors that can appear oppositional to untrained observers

**Student strengths:**
- Has developed genuine resourcefulness and strength from navigating complex circumstances
- When safe, can demonstrate remarkable insight and emotional intelligence
- Responds to adults who show genuine respect and high expectations simultaneously

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Trauma-informed classroom environment: predictable, safe, warm, and shame-free
- Trusted adult relationship is non-negotiable — one consistent mentor
- Flexible academic accommodations during periods of acute trauma exposure
- No public humiliation; all correction is private and restorative
- Center student agency in all educational decision-making

**Assistive technology:**
- Digital communication tools for connecting with trusted adults
- Resource-finding apps for community supports
- Self-regulation apps for use during and after school

**UDL strategies:**
- Trauma-responsive pedagogy school-wide, not just for individual students
- Allow creative and physical expression as valid academic engagement
- Build student agency and leadership throughout the curriculum

**IEP considerations:**
- Transition plan must address real-world survival needs alongside post-secondary goals
- Annual goal: Student will access X community or school support services independently using identified strategies
- Coordinate with probation, child welfare, and housing agencies as part of the IEP team
- Document any punitive school practices that may violate IDEA protections for trauma-related behavior

**Who to collaborate with:** School Social Worker, McKinney-Vento Liaison (if housing-unstable)

**Family partnership tip:** For students in foster care or unstable housing: know your rights under McKinney-Vento and IDEA. Schools must provide records, enrollment, and services regardless of housing status. A social worker can help navigate this.
