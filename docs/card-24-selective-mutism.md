# Selective Mutism

**Category:** Speech & Language  
**IDEA/504 Eligibility:** Speech or Language Impairment (SLI) or Emotional Disturbance (ED) depending on state

## Overview
Selective Mutism is an anxiety disorder in which a student who is able to speak in some settings (typically home) consistently fails to speak in specific social situations (typically school). It is not stubbornness or shyness — it is an anxiety response that overrides speech. Pressure to speak consistently makes selective mutism worse.

---

## Elementary (K–5)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Does not speak at school, or only whispers to one or two peers
- Communicates through nodding, pointing, writing, or pulling others
- Freezes or shows visible distress when asked a direct question
- Participates actively nonverbally — raises hand, completes work, follows directions

**Student strengths:**
- Often highly perceptive and observant
- Participates fully nonverbally — engagement and understanding are typically not impaired
- Often excels in written work when verbal demand is removed

**Recommended accommodations:**
- NEVER require verbal responses; always offer written, pointing, or gesture alternatives
- Do not draw attention to the silence — treat nonverbal communication as fully valid
- Create a private signal system for common classroom needs (bathroom, help, ready to answer)
- Communicate that speaking is never required — this paradoxically reduces anxiety and increases speech
- Implement a gradual exposure plan collaboratively with SLP

**Assistive technology:**
- AAC device or speech-generating app as a bridge communication tool
- Text-to-speech for verbal participation alternatives
- Written response pads or whiteboards for silent communication

**UDL strategies:**
- Provide all participation options in nonverbal formats as the default, not as an exception
- Use written warm-ups and exit tickets instead of verbal check-ins
- Build a classroom culture where many communication modes are used and valued

**IEP considerations:**
- SLP services with an SM-trained therapist are critical — general SLP training is often insufficient
- Annual goal: Student will communicate a need or response using an agreed-upon nonverbal method in X out of Y classroom situations
- A graduated exposure plan (stimulus fading, shaping) should be explicitly documented
- Never use punishment or pressure to force speech — this is harmful and worsens prognosis

**Who to collaborate with:** Speech-Language Pathologist (SM-trained), School Counselor

**Family partnership tip:** At home, practice "brave talking" in school-like situations in low-stakes ways — call to order food, respond to strangers in stores. Graduated exposure at home supports the school plan.

## Middle School (6–8)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Has found workarounds that demonstrate genuine problem-solving and resilience
- Written work is strong; verbal participation is absent or very limited
- Social isolation has deepened as peers move toward more verbal social interaction
- Increasing anxiety around speaking in new situations or with new adults

**Student strengths:**
- Often exceptional writers and communicators in text-based formats
- Has developed profound patience and understanding of communication
- Has genuine insight into their own disability and what supports them

**Recommended accommodations:**
- All participation and assessment in written or nonverbal format
- Individualized plan with SLP: incremental exposure toward verbal communication in targeted settings
- One trusted peer or adult who knows the plan and can bridge communication
- Counseling focused on anxiety, not just speech
- Complete removal of any pressure or consequence for silence

**Assistive technology:**
- Text-to-speech AAC on phone or tablet as a communication tool
- Email or messaging app for communicating with teachers
- Audio or video recordings for assignment responses

**UDL strategies:**
- Make written and digital participation the classroom default for all students
- Allow private submission of questions and responses via email or LMS
- Build student agency into how they demonstrate learning

**IEP considerations:**
- SLP-led graduated exposure plan must be coordinated with all teachers
- Annual goal: Student will initiate communication with a new adult using agreed-upon modality in X out of Y opportunities
- Social isolation should be addressed explicitly as an educational access concern
- Distinguish SM from autism spectrum disorder — different interventions apply

**Who to collaborate with:** Speech-Language Pathologist (SM-trained), School Counselor

**Family partnership tip:** Connect with the Selective Mutism Association for family resources and SLP referrals who specialize in this specific disorder — generic anxiety therapy is often not sufficient.

## High School (9–12)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- May speak in very limited settings but remains nonverbal with most teachers and peers
- Written work demonstrates strong academic ability
- Anxiety about college interviews, presentations, and new social environments
- Has a sophisticated self-understanding of their communication profile

**Student strengths:**
- Often exceptional writers and communicators in text-based formats
- Has developed profound patience and understanding of communication
- Has genuine insight into their own disability and what supports them

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Alternative formats for all oral assessments
- Extended time for oral tasks if chosen
- Private office hours or 1:1 demonstration as alternative to class presentations
- No cold-calling or surprise verbal demands
- Access to SLP services through high school graduation

**Assistive technology:**
- Loom or Screencastify for recorded presentations
- Text-to-speech AAC on phone or tablet as a communication tool
- Email and text-based professional communication training

**UDL strategies:**
- All assignments offer written, recorded, or nonverbal formats
- Transition planning uses written and recorded self-advocacy tools
- Build digital presence and communication skills as a strength

**IEP considerations:**
- Transition goal: Student will communicate their disability and accommodation needs using a written communication tool in two post-secondary settings
- Annual goal: Student will initiate communication with a new adult (outside immediate circle) using agreed-upon modality in X out of Y opportunities
- Summary of Performance must explain the communication profile clearly for college disability services
- Many adults with selective mutism speak more freely in college — document expectations realistically

**Who to collaborate with:** Speech-Language Pathologist, College Guidance Counselor

**Family partnership tip:** Many adults with selective mutism report that college was a turning point — the environment changed and they began speaking more. Don't assume high school is the permanent picture. Keep supporting and keep hope.
