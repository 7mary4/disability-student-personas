# Social Communication Disorder

**Category:** Speech & Language  
**IDEA/504 Eligibility:** Speech or Language Impairment (SLI)

## Overview
Social Communication Disorder (SCD) affects the pragmatic use of language — the social rules of communication including turn-taking, staying on topic, adjusting language to the listener, and interpreting implied meaning. Students with SCD often have normal vocabulary and grammar but use language in ways that are socially awkward or confusing. It is distinct from but often confused with autism.

---

## Elementary (K–5)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Dominates conversations with monologues about their topic
- Interrupts; doesn't wait for natural pauses to contribute
- Doesn't adjust communication style to different listeners (same with teacher as with peers)
- Misses implied meaning, sarcasm, and the "point" of jokes

**Student strengths:**
- Often genuine, direct, and honest in communication
- Strong structural language — can write well even if pragmatics are weak
- Often well-liked by adults even when peer relationships are difficult

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Explicit instruction in conversational rules (turn-taking, topic maintenance, social cues)
- Visual cues or scripts for social situations
- Structured peer interaction formats that scaffold turn-taking
- Private feedback on social communication (never public correction)
- Pre-teach the pragmatic expectations for new social situations

**Assistive technology:**
- Social stories apps for situational social scripts
- Video self-modeling for reviewing conversational behavior
- Conversation prompt cards for use in social situations

**UDL strategies:**
- Teach hidden social curriculum explicitly — what conversations are supposed to look like
- Use role-play and video examples to teach pragmatic rules
- Create structured social interaction opportunities with clear roles

**IEP considerations:**
- SLP services are primary — pragmatic language is an SLP domain
- Annual goal: Student will maintain a two-exchange conversation (listen → respond → ask question) with a peer in X out of Y structured opportunities
- Distinguish SCD from autism spectrum disorder — different profile, different interventions
- Social skills goals should be contextualized — what specific skills in what specific settings?

**Who to collaborate with:** Speech-Language Pathologist, School Counselor

**Family partnership tip:** Watch a movie together and pause to ask "What does that character mean by that?" Discussing implied communication in safe, fictional contexts builds real pragmatic language skills.

## Middle School (6–8)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Social relationships are deteriorating as peer communication becomes more complex
- May be seen as rude, inappropriate, or "weird" without understanding why
- Academic language use (persuasion, inference, implied meaning in text) is difficult
- Group work is a significant challenge due to social communication breakdowns

**Student strengths:**
- Often genuine, direct, and honest in communication
- Strong structural language — can write well even if pragmatics are weak
- Often well-liked by adults even when peer relationships are difficult

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Social skills group focused on pragmatic language (not generic social-emotional learning)
- Structured role assignment in all group work
- SLP-supported analysis of social situations that went wrong
- Private debrief after social situations with trusted adult
- Explicit rubric for participation expectations in group activities

**Assistive technology:**
- Structured communication apps for practice
- Video self-modeling for reviewing conversational behavior
- Written communication channel with teachers for self-advocacy

**UDL strategies:**
- Teach pragmatic language explicitly across the curriculum
- Use video clips of effective communication as teaching tools
- Build structured peer interaction with explicit social roles

**IEP considerations:**
- SLP services should address academic pragmatics, not just social conversation
- Annual goal: Student will use turn-taking and topic maintenance in a structured 5-minute peer conversation in X out of Y opportunities
- Address social isolation as an educational issue — it affects engagement and wellbeing
- Bullying victimization prevention should be addressed if relevant

**Who to collaborate with:** Speech-Language Pathologist, School Counselor

**Family partnership tip:** Watch a movie together and pause to ask "What does that character mean by that?" Discussing implied communication in safe, fictional contexts builds real pragmatic language skills.

## High School (9–12)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Job interviews, college interviews, and presentations are particularly challenging
- Professional emails and written communication may be too direct or unclear in pragmatic tone
- Peer social life is limited but student may have one or two solid friendships
- Has often developed significant self-awareness about their communication profile

**Student strengths:**
- Often genuine, direct, and honest — qualities valued in many professional contexts
- Has often developed strong one-on-one relationship skills
- Self-aware and developing explicit knowledge about their communication needs

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Explicit preparation for college interviews and job application processes
- Coaching on written communication tone for professional contexts
- Social skills coaching focused on workplace and post-secondary contexts
- Flexible participation options for group discussions
- Alternative to in-person interviews where possible (written application components)

**Assistive technology:**
- Email templates and structured communication tools for professional contexts
- Video interview practice platforms
- Grammarly for written communication tone feedback

**UDL strategies:**
- Teach self-advocacy explicitly: how to disclose SCD in appropriate contexts
- Build professional communication practice into advisory and transition curriculum
- Use video interviews as practice for real post-secondary communication demands

**IEP considerations:**
- Transition goals must address post-secondary communication contexts: workplace, college
- Annual goal: Student will demonstrate awareness of communication needs by independently using two SCD-specific strategies during a simulated interview or group discussion
- Summary of Performance should include communication profile for post-secondary disability services
- SLP services may transition to coaching model for post-secondary preparation

**Who to collaborate with:** Speech-Language Pathologist, College Guidance Counselor

**Family partnership tip:** Help your student practice professional communication at home — writing emails to teachers, practicing phone calls, discussing workplace scenarios. Explicit rehearsal transfers to real contexts.
