# Expressive Language Disorder

**Category:** Speech & Language  
**IDEA/504 Eligibility:** Speech or Language Impairment (SLI)

## Overview
Expressive Language Disorder affects a student's ability to produce language — to find words, form grammatically correct sentences, organize ideas verbally or in writing, and communicate effectively. These students understand far more than they can express. They are often misidentified as having low ability when the real issue is an output bottleneck, not comprehension.

---

## Elementary (K–5)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Understands everything said but struggles to put words together in response
- Uses simple or incorrect sentence structures; has word-finding difficulties ("that thing... you know...")
- Written and verbal output doesn't reflect actual understanding
- May be reluctant to speak in class or participate in discussions

**Student strengths:**
- Rich comprehension and receptive language skills
- Often strong listener and observer who absorbs content deeply
- Motivated to communicate when given appropriate tools and time

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Provide additional wait time when calling on the student (10+ seconds)
- Allow alternative response formats: drawing, pointing, gesture, or writing
- Offer sentence starters and word banks for verbal and written responses
- Use graphic organizers to help organize ideas before speaking or writing
- Never finish the student's sentences — wait patiently

**Assistive technology:**
- Speech-to-text tools to capture ideas faster than manual writing
- Word prediction apps (Co:Writer) to scaffold written expression
- AAC as a supplement if verbal expression is significantly limited

**UDL strategies:**
- Assess content knowledge through multiple modalities: drawing, labeling, oral recording
- Teach academic language explicitly — vocabulary for persuasion, explanation, comparison
- Use think-time protocols before any verbal or written production

**IEP considerations:**
- Annual goal: Student will use a graphic organizer to produce a structured 3-sentence response to academic questions in X out of Y writing tasks
- SLP goals should address academic language and discourse, not just sentence-level production
- Coordinate SLP goals with ELA teacher to ensure generalization to academic contexts
- Distinguish expressive language disorder from bilingual language development if ELL status is present

**Who to collaborate with:** Speech-Language Pathologist, ELA Teacher

**Family partnership tip:** Encourage dinner table conversation that requires full sentences — "Tell me three things that happened today" instead of "How was school?" Practice in low-stakes, warm contexts builds expressive language over time.

## Middle School (6–8)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Oral responses are brief, grammatically simple, or disorganized compared to peers
- Written work is far shorter and less detailed than comprehension would predict
- May avoid volunteering answers even when they know the material
- Word-finding difficulties become more apparent in complex academic language

**Student strengths:**
- Strong receptive understanding and content knowledge
- Often perceptive and observant — takes in a great deal even when saying little
- Persistent and highly motivated when given appropriate scaffolds

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Extended time for verbal responses; do not cold-call without advance notice
- Allow written or recorded alternatives to spoken responses
- Provide structured templates for all oral and written academic tasks
- SLP pull-out or co-teaching for academic language support
- Reduce the language load on assessments where possible (multiple choice, labeled diagrams)

**Assistive technology:**
- Speech-to-text for all written assignments
- Vocabulary apps for academic word building
- Otter.ai for transcribing verbal drafts

**UDL strategies:**
- Use structured academic conversations with sentence frames provided
- Teach content-specific vocabulary explicitly before each unit
- Allow students to demonstrate knowledge through labeled diagrams, models, or visual maps

**IEP considerations:**
- SLP services should continue through middle school — language demands increase significantly
- Annual goal: Student will produce a structured academic response of 5+ sentences using a graphic organizer in X out of Y opportunities
- Coordinate between SLP, ELA, and content teachers for consistent language scaffolding
- Document expressive language accommodations for state testing

**Who to collaborate with:** Speech-Language Pathologist, ELA Teacher

**Family partnership tip:** Ask your student's SLP for specific activities to do at home that match what's being worked on in therapy. Consistency between home and school accelerates progress significantly.

## High School (9–12)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- College application essays and formal writing are significantly below the student's actual knowledge
- Oral presentations are stressful and below verbal peer norms even with preparation
- May have strong content knowledge that is never fully visible in assessed work
- Has developed coping strategies (careful preparation, written alternatives) that work inconsistently

**Student strengths:**
- Strong content knowledge and deep comprehension that shows in discussion when supported
- Has developed persistence and self-awareness about communication needs
- Often skilled in visual, creative, or technical areas that don't rely on verbal expression

**Recommended accommodations:**
- All major assessments available in alternative formats (recorded, diagrammed, written)
- Explicit instruction in academic writing structures (thesis, evidence, analysis)
- SLP support for college application writing and interview preparation
- Extended time on all written and oral assessments
- Allow use of notes and graphic organizers during all presentations

**Assistive technology:**
- Dragon NaturallySpeaking or Google Docs voice typing for extended writing
- Grammarly for editing and sentence structure feedback
- Mind-mapping tools for organizing ideas before writing or presenting

**UDL strategies:**
- Provide structured templates for all academic writing
- Allow portfolio demonstration of learning over time rather than single-sitting assessments
- Teach self-advocacy: how to ask for clarification or extended response time

**IEP considerations:**
- SLP services may be continued or transitioned to self-directed use of strategies
- Transition planning must address communication needs in college and workplace contexts
- Annual goal: Student will independently produce a structured written or recorded argument of X sentences using a planning tool in Y% of assignments
- Document for college disability services the specific nature of the expressive language deficit

**Who to collaborate with:** Speech-Language Pathologist, College Guidance Counselor

**Family partnership tip:** Help your student practice using their assistive technology at home for everyday writing tasks — texts, emails, notes. The habit of using tools builds fluency before they need them in high-stakes settings.
