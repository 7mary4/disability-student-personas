# Deaf/Hard of Hearing

**Category:** Sensory & Physical  
**IDEA/504 Eligibility:** Hearing Impairment (HI) including Deafness

## Overview
Students who are Deaf or hard of hearing experience a wide range of hearing loss that may affect speech perception, language development, academic learning, and social interaction. Communication modalities vary widely — some students use spoken language with hearing aids or cochlear implants, others use American Sign Language (ASL), and many use a combination. Educational planning must consider the student's communication mode, language, and Deaf community identity.

---

## Elementary (K–5)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Misses oral instructions, especially when background noise is present
- Watches faces and lips carefully; may struggle when the teacher turns away
- May have speech that is difficult to understand; misses sounds they cannot hear
- Fatigue from the sustained effort of listening and lip-reading

**Student strengths:**
- Often highly visual learner; strong visual attention and observation skills
- Develops strong written communication skills with appropriate support
- Resilient and adaptive from navigating a hearing world

**Recommended accommodations:**
- FM system or sound-field amplification in the classroom
- Face the student when speaking; ensure clear line of sight
- Provide all instructions in written form in addition to spoken
- Seat the student where they have optimal line of sight to teacher and peers
- Reduce background noise in the classroom (carpet, door closed, white noise minimized)

**Assistive technology:**
- FM system (teacher-worn microphone transmitting to student receiver)
- Captioning tools for all video content
- Visual alert systems (flashing lights for fire alarms, announcements)

**UDL strategies:**
- Pair all verbal instruction with written or visual support as standard practice
- Use visual communication in addition to speech for key information
- Ensure all classroom media (videos, announcements) are captioned

**IEP considerations:**
- Teacher of the Deaf/Hard of Hearing (TODHH) must be on the IEP team
- IDEA requires consideration of the student's communication mode, language, and Deaf community opportunities
- Annual goal may address listening skills, language development, reading, or self-advocacy
- Audiological data (audiogram, aided hearing thresholds) must be current in the IEP

**Who to collaborate with:** Teacher of the Deaf/Hard of Hearing, Educational Interpreter (if applicable)

**Family partnership tip:** Learn American Sign Language alongside your child if they use it. Families who sign together with their Deaf child have dramatically better outcomes. Even beginner signs matter enormously.

## Middle School (6–8)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Navigating both academic demands and social dynamics in a world designed for hearing people
- Group conversations in noisy settings (cafeteria, hallway) are particularly challenging
- May be experiencing identity questions about Deaf identity and hearing world membership
- Interpreter fatigue (watching interpreter for extended periods is cognitively demanding)

**Student strengths:**
- Often deeply invested in visual, written, and non-verbal communication
- Part of a rich cultural and linguistic community if identity is affirmed
- Strong visual attention and nonverbal communication skills

**Recommended accommodations:**
- Educational interpreter or CART services for all academic settings
- Preferential seating with optimal sight lines to interpreter, teacher, and peers
- All video content captioned; no uncaptioned media
- Extended time on reading-heavy tasks (simultaneous visual processing for interpreting or lip-reading is tiring)
- Access to note-taker or digital notes to reduce dual-task demands

**Assistive technology:**
- CART (Communication Access Realtime Translation) for complex academic settings
- Video relay service for phone-based communication
- Captioned telephone and visual alert systems

**UDL strategies:**
- Make captioning and visual supports the classroom default for all students
- Teach hearing peers about Deaf culture and communication — build classroom community
- Allow processing time between sign/caption interpretation and student response

**IEP considerations:**
- Address bullying and social victimization explicitly if occurring
- Annual goal may address academic language in the student's primary language (ASL or English)
- Evaluate LRE at each IEP — school of the Deaf may be appropriate for some students
- Coordinate ASL instruction and Deaf cultural identity development as IEP components

**Who to collaborate with:** Teacher of the Deaf/Hard of Hearing, Educational Interpreter

**Family partnership tip:** Connect your student with the Deaf community — Deaf mentors, Deaf clubs, and Deaf events provide the language modeling, identity, and peer community that hearing schools often cannot fully provide.

## High School (9–12)

**In your classroom you might notice:**
- Self-managed AT and interpreter/CART requests
- Accessible format for SAT/ACT and AP exams
- Career exploration that includes Deaf-friendly and accessible workplaces
- Senior year transition planning including interpreter booking and CART request practice

**Student strengths:**
- Often bilingual in ASL and English — a genuine and valuable skill
- Strong self-advocacy and understanding of communication access rights
- Has navigated complex communication environments and developed remarkable flexibility

**Recommended accommodations:**
- CART for all college-level coursework
- VideoPhone and ASL-accessible communication platforms
- Captioned telephone services for voice calls
- Self-advocacy preparation for college disability services
- Career exploration that includes Deaf-accessible work environments

**Assistive technology:**
- CART for all college-level coursework
- VideoPhone and ASL-accessible communication platforms
- Captioned telephone services for voice calls

**UDL strategies:**
- Teach self-advocacy for access needs as explicit curriculum
- Connect student with Gallaudet, NTID, or Deaf post-secondary program resources
- Develop disability disclosure script for employers and professors

**IEP considerations:**
- Transition plan must address: post-secondary education (interpreter/CART access, Deaf-friendly campuses), employment (ADA interpreter rights), and independent living
- Annual goal: Student will independently request and confirm interpreter/CART services for X post-secondary settings
- Summary of Performance must include communication profile, technology in use, and documentation for post-secondary disability services
- Vocational Rehabilitation referral for pre-employment transition services

**Who to collaborate with:** Transition Coordinator, Teacher of the Deaf/Hard of Hearing, VR Counselor

**Family partnership tip:** Research post-secondary options that have strong Deaf services — Gallaudet University, NTID at RIT, and many state universities have robust Deaf student communities. The right environment changes everything.
